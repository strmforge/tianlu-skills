# Source-Grounded Structured Data Readiness Review

Status: incubating.

## Signal

External feeds increasingly frame two adjacent needs:

- Documents, files, screenshots, spreadsheets, websites, and long reports need to become typed structured data with source pointers, confidence notes, nulls for missing fields, and reviewable exceptions.
- Businesses want to be visible in generative search or agentic browsing surfaces, but official guidance still routes this through source quality, crawlability, indexability, clear technical structure, and ordinary SEO fundamentals rather than a guaranteed "AI ranking" trick.

Open-source and vendor tools can help parse, convert, extract, or analyze files, but tool capability is not proof that a solo operator can charge for the work or that the output is correct enough for production use.

## Proposed Trigger

Use this candidate when a user asks about:

- turning messy files, PDFs, office documents, emails, images, audio/video transcripts, forms, invoices, contracts, reports, or websites into structured data;
- creating a cited source map, schema, extraction QA packet, document-intelligence brief, AI-search readiness checklist, or owner handoff;
- evaluating whether document extraction, web extraction, local business AI-search visibility, citation readiness, or structured presence can become a one-person-one-computer service offer.

## Proposed Mechanism

1. Separate the job from the claim.
   - The useful job is source readiness, extraction design, QA, and owner handoff.
   - Do not treat tool demos, repo stars, launch posts, or AI-search anecdotes as buyer proof, accuracy proof, ranking proof, or income proof.
2. Classify paid-proof evidence before shaping the offer.
   - L0: personal social claim, founder post, launch post, screenshot, reply, course page, or unverifiable "clients are paying" statement.
   - L1: public service page, public pricing page, intake form, lead magnet, demo funnel, agency offer, product page, or open-source workflow aimed at agencies/operators.
   - L2: checkout-readback, public customer case, signed case study, public usage or revenue report, or owner-supplied billing evidence reviewed inside a scoped private engagement.
   - A public price or service page proves offer existence, not customer payment, margin, retention, ranking impact, or solo-operator viability.
3. Treat readiness scores as source-indexed summaries, not proof.
   - Separate directly observed technical signals, sampled model responses, public mention or citation evidence, research-backed hypotheses, vendor/tool inference, and unknown or missing data.
   - Require each score band or priority to point back to source rows, prompt/model samples, or explicit "not verified" notes.
   - Do not infer backlink strength, branded demand, citation share, AI visibility, revenue impact, or customer payment from page HTML, a single model run, a vendor score, a social claim, or a public pricing page.
   - Source-audit vendor research pages and scoring frameworks before relying on them; cited papers, official search guidance, internal analyses, and marketing interpretation are different evidence types.
4. Build the source inventory.
   - List each document, page, URL, file type, owner, version/date, access boundary, sensitivity, and intended downstream use.
   - Mark missing, stale, private, controlled, regulated, or unavailable sources explicitly.
5. Define the schema and evidence contract.
   - For each target field, record definition, source pointer, allowed null behavior, confidence status, validation rule, exception condition, and reviewer need.
   - Prefer small samples before bulk processing.
6. Treat extraction tools as assistants, not authority.
   - Record tool/vendor/repo, version or access date, input files, transformation path, unsupported formats, and known security limits.
   - Sample-audit outputs against source documents, especially tables, totals, dates, names, clauses, OCR text, and computed values.
7. For AI-search or agentic-discovery readiness, stay inside official search boundaries.
   - Check crawlability, robots directives, indexability, canonical pages, clear content, local business or ecommerce details, source citations, and ordinary structured data eligibility.
   - Do not claim ranking, recommendation placement, AI citation inclusion, lead generation, revenue, compliance, or platform acceptance.
8. Package a human-owned handoff.
   - Output source inventory, schema, extraction sample, issue ledger, confidence/null table, QA sample, risk caveats, and owner decisions.
   - Do not upload private files, connect accounts, submit forms, edit websites, change robots, publish content, write CRM/CMS/PIM records, or retain sensitive data without scoped approval.

## Initial Scope

This candidate is for readiness review, source mapping, extraction QA, and service-offer framing. It does not authorize production extraction, data migration, website publication, SEO guarantee, AI-search guarantee, legal/compliance/accounting conclusions, account actions, customer contact, payment, installation, or durable retention of private data.

## Known Failure Modes

- Turning a vendor or open-source capability list into proof that a business can be sold.
- Treating structured data, citations, confidence scores, or RAG language as proof of correctness.
- Losing source pointers while transforming files into markdown, JSON, or tables.
- Trusting OCR, table extraction, arithmetic, or computed fields without sample audit.
- Mixing private, regulated, or customer data into a generic tool without an explicit data-handling boundary.
- Promising AI-search ranking, ChatGPT recommendation inclusion, Google AI Overview placement, leads, revenue, compliance, or production readiness.
- Treating a GEO, AI-visibility, or structured-data score as proof without source pointers, sample methodology, counterexamples, and missing-data labels.
- Accepting a vendor research page as primary evidence without checking the cited paper, official source, benchmark scope, and whether an internal analysis has been mixed with peer-reviewed or official guidance.
- Installing, connecting, uploading, downloading, publishing, or writing to systems based on a feed signal.

## Verification Needed

- Replay on small, public document sets where source fields can be checked by hand.
- Replay on website readiness reviews where official search documentation can validate the allowed checklist and reject ranking guarantees.
- For public-only website dry-runs, record the target URL, fetched public pages, HTTP status/source-gap table, observed metadata/headings/links/structured-data status, public entity/source evidence, and a no-account/no-write/no-contact boundary before recommending owner actions.
- Compare local/offline conversion tools, hosted APIs, and manual extraction on the same sample for source-pointer retention and error patterns.
- Replay against public pricing pages, agency service pages, and personal paid-client claims to test whether the method separates offer existence from paid proof.
- Replay scorecards against counterexamples: high score with no citation, low score with citation, schema-complete pages with weak visibility, and single-page optimization that fails to beat a baseline.
- Add eval coverage only if repeated failures show that existing evidence, browser, MCP/tool, and solo-business boundaries are insufficient.
