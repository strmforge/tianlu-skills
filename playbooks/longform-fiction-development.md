# Playbook: Long-Form Fiction Development

Status: seed draft

Scope: creative-writing-neutral. This playbook helps an agent develop fiction artifacts. It is not tied to one genre, publishing platform, fandom, author, or style.

## Purpose

Help an agent turn a story intention into reusable creative artifacts: premise, audience promise, character sheets, world rules, conflict map, chapter outline, draft plan, continuity tracker, and revision notes.

## Inputs

- Seed idea, theme, image, premise, scene, or emotional target.
- Intended audience and reading experience.
- Genre or comparison works, if available.
- Desired length, format, cadence, and style constraints.
- Existing notes, scenes, characters, world details, or prior chapters.
- Author preferences and non-goals.

## Procedure

1. Position the story.
   - Turn the seed idea into a premise, genre signal, audience promise, and creative boundary.
   - Name what the story should make the reader feel.

2. Build character engines.
   - For each major character, define want, need, fear, pressure, contradiction, relationship function, and change path.
   - Avoid making characters only carry plot instructions.

3. Define world rules.
   - Record social, physical, magical, technological, historical, or institutional rules that affect choices.
   - Tie rules to conflict instead of building unused background.

4. Design conflict.
   - Define the central opposition, stakes, time pressure, tradeoffs, and escalation path.
   - Split conflict into stages and scene-level pressures.

5. Outline chapters or episodes.
   - Assign each chapter a function: reveal, choice, reversal, confrontation, intimacy, setup, payoff, or recovery.
   - Track hooks and pacing so the outline is not only a list of events.

6. Track continuity and foreshadowing.
   - Maintain a register of facts, promises, clues, secrets, and payoffs.
   - Mark unresolved threads before drafting new material.
   - Treat a story bible or continuity ledger as a living working artifact, not a rulebook that freezes every creative choice.
   - Separate confirmed canon, tentative ideas, unknowns, retcons, and change notes.

7. Draft and revise.
   - Draft against the chapter function and emotional target.
   - Revise for causality, voice, pacing, continuity, and reader promise before polishing style.

## Outputs

- Story positioning note.
- Character sheets and relationship map.
- World rule sheet.
- Conflict ladder.
- Chapter or episode outline.
- Foreshadowing and payoff register.
- Story bible or continuity ledger when the project is long, serialized, or revision-heavy.
- Retcon and change log.
- Draft plan and revision checklist.

## Quality Signals

- The premise has a clear reader promise.
- Character choices drive plot movement.
- World rules create or constrain conflict.
- Each chapter changes the state of the story.
- Payoffs trace back to earlier setup.
- Continuity records protect future writing without turning creative exploration into approval work.
- Revision notes distinguish structural issues from prose polish.

## Human Checkpoints

- Final creative direction, taste, tone, taboo boundaries, and genre promise.
- Major plot turns such as death, betrayal, romance outcome, ending, or theme inversion.
- Publication decisions and any use of copyrighted source material or living-person likeness.
- Durable story-state writes that should affect future sessions or collaborators.

Known failures:

- Treating genre conventions as fixed law.
