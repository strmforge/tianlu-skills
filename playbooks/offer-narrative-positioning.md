# Playbook: Offer And Narrative Positioning

Status: seed draft

Scope: domain-neutral. This playbook helps an agent turn audience evidence, product or story intent, and constraints into a value proposition and narrative positioning package. It is not tied to one marketplace, content platform, brand, genre, campaign, or sales method.

## Purpose

Help an agent produce a positioning brief, value proposition ladder, narrative message house, audience-objection sheet, channel adaptation kit, and claim-risk flags.

For commerce work, use this playbook before product conversion assets when the main question is buyer promise, differentiation, proof, objection handling, or risky claim language. Then compose with `commerce-operations.md` and `asset-repurposing-pipeline.md` for product-page, media, FAQ, ad, email, or short-form variants.

## Inputs

- Audience, reader, buyer, user, stakeholder, or community segment.
- Problem, job, desire, pain, gain, or promised reader experience.
- Product, service, story, offer, creator, feature, campaign, or idea to position.
- Evidence such as research notes, feedback, reviews, interviews, performance data, competitive notes, or source brief.
- Constraints such as brand voice, author voice, legal limits, claim policy, pricing, channel format, or taboo boundaries.
- Desired action: understand, trust, try, buy, subscribe, read, share, approve, or give feedback.

## Procedure

1. Define the audience and action.
   - State who the positioning is for, what they are trying to accomplish, and what action or belief the narrative should support.
   - Name non-goals and audiences that are out of scope.

2. Map the audience problem.
   - Record jobs, pains, gains, objections, alternatives, and current workarounds.
   - Separate evidence-backed observations from assumptions.

3. Build the value proof chain.
   - Connect the offer or story to the audience problem: claim, supporting point, evidence type, proof status, and limitation.
   - Mark unsupported, regulated, comparative, superlative, or sensitive claims.

4. Choose the positioning angle.
   - Compare several candidate angles by audience fit, differentiation, credibility, specificity, and risk.
   - Reject angles that depend on fabricated scarcity, fake proof, hidden manipulation, or misleading comparisons.

5. Draft the narrative package.
   - Produce a one-line promise, short narrative, long narrative, supporting points, objection responses, and channel adaptation notes.
   - Keep facts, interpretations, aspirations, and rhetorical language distinguishable.

6. Adapt for use cases.
   - Translate the positioning into commerce copy, content angle, creator bio, story pitch, product brief, landing section, or internal alignment note as needed.
   - Preserve the core promise while changing format and emphasis.

7. Review claims and risks.
   - Create claim-risk flags and human review notes before publication, launch, sales use, or regulated-domain communication.
   - Record what evidence would strengthen or weaken the positioning.

## Outputs

- Positioning brief.
- Value proposition ladder.
- Narrative message house.
- Audience and objection handling sheet.
- Channel adaptation kit.
- Claim-risk flag list.
- Review questions and next-action notes.

## Quality Signals

- The core promise answers who it is for, what changes for them, and why it is credible.
- Important claims link to evidence or are clearly marked as assumptions or aspirations.
- The narrative can change format without changing its core promise.
- Objections and misunderstandings are anticipated, not hidden.
- The output avoids fabricated proof, fake scarcity, fake testimonials, and unsupported performance claims.
- A reviewer can approve, revise, or reject each claim and channel variant.

## Human Checkpoints

- Final brand, author, creator, or public positioning.
- Publication, launch, sales use, ad use, customer reply, or external send.
- Legal, medical, financial, safety, political, minor-related, or regulated claims.
- Comparative, superlative, testimonial, endorsement, performance, or outcome claims.
- Use of private feedback, user-generated content, confidential strategy, or competitor-sensitive information.

## Evidence

Source signal: cross-domain review, value proposition methods, and feed sampling showed a recurring gap between research evidence and usable external narrative.

Validation state: seed draft. Needs replay on real commerce, content, creative-writing, and internal-alignment tasks before promotion.

Known failures:

- Treating positioning language as verified fact.
- Producing catchy copy that cannot be traced to audience evidence or a real offer.
- Reusing the same narrative across channels without accounting for audience and format.
- Making claims that require legal, medical, financial, or other domain review without flagging them.
